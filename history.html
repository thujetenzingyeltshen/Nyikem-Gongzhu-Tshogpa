History

Who Is a Nyikem?

A fuller historical note on the meaning of Nyikem, the honorific title of Dasho, the red scarf tradition in Bhutan, and the foundations that later shaped the formation of Nyikem Gongzhu Tshogpa.

Dasho Zangley Dukpa

Historical background of Nyikem and Dasho

Background

Meaning of Nyikem and Dasho

The document explains that Nyikelm literally carries the sense of double rank or double entitlement. With the conferment of the red scarf by His Majesty the King, a person becomes Nyikelm, and this distinction is closely associated with the honorific title Dasho.

The article adds an older historical explanation for the term. In medieval Bhutan, a Nyikelm was said to be entitled to two riding ponies, while certain other senior officials such as Chipzhon were entitled to one. This is one of the ways the idea of "double" entitlement is understood in the historical record.

Historical accounts cited in the document point to earlier usages of the title Dasho in Bhutan. A biography connected to Hungrel Drung Drung of Paro mentions Dashos in Paro even before the arrival of Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal in Bhutan in 1616 CE. The article also refers to Dr. John Ardussi's observation of titles such as Dasho Jangsapa, suggesting that Dasho was already linked with powerful patrons and persons of standing.

At the same time, the author is careful to note that there is still no single comprehensive research work devoted entirely to the title of Nyikem and its honorific Dasho. The article therefore pieces together its account from published Bhutanese sources, translated legal texts, and personal interaction with retired and serving public servants.

Historical Notes

Kabne, Rank, and Earlier Governance

The document traces the symbolic background of the red scarf through Bhutanese discussions of kabne. Gyalzim Dasho Dorji Gyaltshen writes that the use of different robes and scarves was tied to the display of respect, and the article explains that this culture of differentiated dress later became part of Bhutan's public order and official hierarchy.

It further notes that during the reign of Zhabdrung Ngawang Namgyal, senior lay officials selected for state service came to wear different colours according to title and position. In this setting, officials of higher civil standing, including red-scarf officers, became visibly distinguished within the structure of governance.

01

Traditional writings describe kabne as a visible expression of respect, discipline, and ordered status in Bhutanese society.

02

Accounts from the Zhabdrung period describe different scarf colours being associated with different official positions, including ministers and red-scarf officers.

03

Lopon Nado's account describes nine high state ministers in temporal administration, while other dzongpons and senior officials generally wore red scarves according to rank.

04

Texts cited in the article describe Nyikelma as a higher entitlement than Chipzhon, including references to servants, a horseman, and the broader idea of double rank.

The article also quotes John Ardussi and Karma Ura on the composition of the governing structure in earlier Bhutan. Their discussion supports the idea that red-scarf officials included dzongpons, nyerchens, zimpons, and mgron-nyers of each dzong, while Chipzhon officials were understood to receive only part of the perquisites associated with the red-scarf rank.

A further legal reference appears in the translated Discourse on Legal Decree of Gongsa Mipham Wangpo, where section 93 is cited as assigning specific attendants to two-fold officials. In the article, this is used as another historical indication that Nyikelm referred not simply to ceremony, but to recognized administrative standing and entitlement.

Social Meaning

Title, Service, and Public Perception

The document does not present Nyikem only as an old administrative title. It also reflects on how the title of Dasho is understood in modern society. The author refers to a public debate, including online discussion, in which some people saw Dasho as an increasingly visible social status marker and others defended its deeper meaning as recognition earned through service.

To make that point clearer, the article compares the Bhutanese discussion with title cultures elsewhere, including the Datukship in Malaysia and knighthood in the United Kingdom. These comparisons are not used to say the systems are identical, but to show that societies often attach prestige, aspiration, and social expectations to honors granted by the state or sovereign.

The article also quotes Karma Phuntsho's discussion of the modern civil service after 1973, when the growth of administrative service changed social aspiration in Bhutan. In that context, the post of Dasho became a much-desired ambition, showing how public office, education, and honour became connected in the modern period.

Yet the central argument of the document remains clear: the conferment of red scarves by the Druk Gyalpo is fundamentally tied to loyalty, dedication, patriotism, professionalism, extraordinary leadership, and distinguished public service to the Tsa-Wa-Sum. In that sense, the title is not merely ceremonial, but a public recognition of trust, merit, and national contribution.

NGT

Establishment of Nyikem Gongzhu Tshogpa

According to the document, Nyikem Gongzhu Tshogpa was established on 26 May 2013 under the Command of His Majesty the Druk Gyalpo. This exact date matters because the article presents NGT not as a general social association, but as an institution formed through a specific royal command and national purpose.

The rationale is preserved in the foreword quoted from Lyonpo C. Dorji, Chairman of the Privy Council, dated 7 October 2013. The passage says that the association was established in recognition of the outstanding services rendered to the Tsa-Wa-Sum by former Nyikem officers and the wealth of knowledge and experience they possess.

The document also records a second important command: members of NGT were to volunteer and informally share their experience and knowledge with schools and other institutions of learning. This means the Tshogpa was envisioned not only as an honor-based body, but also as a living national resource.

The history of NGT therefore combines recognition with responsibility. It honours distinguished service already rendered, while ensuring that the wisdom, memory, and practical experience of former Nyikem officers continue to benefit younger generations and the wider nation.

Continuity

Why This History Matters

Read as a whole, the document links several layers of Bhutanese public life: older ideas of rank and entitlement, the symbolic meaning of kabne, the historical role of senior officers in governance, the modern meaning of professional public service, and the creation of NGT as a way to preserve experience in service of the country.

That continuity is what gives the history page its purpose. Nyikem is not described only as a title to be admired; it is presented as a tradition of responsibility, dignity, and contribution that culminates in the continuing work of Nyikem Gongzhu Tshogpa.
